# Action Plan Template
### Research-Backed Execution Framework | PMI · McKinsey · Harvard · Eisenhower · RACI · SMART · MoSCoW

> **Purpose of this template:**
> To translate strategy into tasks that are executable, measurable, and trackable.
> Strategy answers *what we want* — the action plan answers *how, when, and who*.
>
> Recommended review cycle: **Weekly** for tasks | **Monthly** for initiatives | **Quarterly** for the full plan.

## 2. Define Goals Using SMART Criteria

**What to write:**
For every major goal in this plan, write it in SMART format. Vague goals produce vague tasks — and vague tasks never get done.

> *(Source: PMI PMBOK® Guide; Morningmate Project Management Guide, 2025)*

| SMART Criterion | The Question It Answers | ✗ Weak Example | ✓ Strong Example |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Specific** | What exactly do we want to achieve? | "Improve sales" | "Increase Product X sales in the Northern region" |
| **Measurable** | How will we know we've achieved it? | "A noticeable increase" | "By 20%" |
| **Achievable** | Is it realistic given available resources? | "Double revenue in one month" | "Grow 20% this quarter" |
| **Relevant** | Does it serve the strategic objectives? | A goal disconnected from OKRs | A goal directly tied to OKR #1 |
| **Time-bound** | What is the deadline? | "As soon as possible" | "By end of Q3 — September 30" |

---

## 3. Prioritization — Eisenhower Matrix + MoSCoW

**What to write:**
Before assigning tasks, classify your initiatives. Having too many priorities means having no priorities.

> Organizations attempting to execute more than five major strategic initiatives simultaneously see sharply lower success rates. Capacity — not ambition — is the binding constraint.
> *(Source: Gartner — Strategic Execution Research)*

### 3.1 The Eisenhower Matrix — Daily & Weekly Prioritization
*(Source: Dwight D. Eisenhower; popularized by Stephen Covey in "The 7 Habits of Highly Effective People")*

| | **Urgent** | **Not Urgent** |
|---|---|---|
| **Important** | **Q1 — Do First:** Crises, pressing deadlines, immediate problems | **Q2 — Schedule:** Planning, relationship-building, strategic development ← **This is where most time should go** |
| **Not Important** | **Q3 — Delegate:** Meetings and requests others can handle | **Q4 — Eliminate:** Distractions that deliver no value |

> 💡 The golden test: Ask two questions for every task — Does it have a hard deadline within 48 hours with material consequences? Does it directly advance one of your quarterly OKRs? If it fails both, it doesn't belong in Q1.
> *(Source: Deckary — "Eisenhower Matrix Guide", former McKinsey & Deloitte consultant, 2026)*

### 3.2 The MoSCoW Method — Initiative-Level Prioritization
*(Source: Dynamic Systems Development Method — DSDM; widely adopted in PMI and Agile frameworks)*

| Classification | Definition | What to Write Here |
|---|---|---|
| **Must Have** | Non-negotiable — failure to deliver means the plan fails | Critical initiatives that protect the core business or fulfill strategic commitments |
| **Should Have** | Very important but survivable if briefly delayed | High-impact initiatives that strengthen results |
| **Could Have** | Desirable if time and resources allow | Improvements and good ideas that aren't critical |
| **Won't Have** | Out of scope for this cycle — a conscious decision, not an oversight | What you've decided NOT to do now, to avoid distraction |

> ⚠️ Critical constraint: Do not allocate more than 60% of capacity to **Must Haves**. If everything is "must have," nothing actually is.
> *(Source: Fluidwave — "Task Prioritization Techniques", 2026)*

## 5. Responsibility Matrix — RACI

**What to write:**
For every major initiative, define who does what. Role ambiguity is the most common cause of execution failure.

> **RACI stands for:**
> - **R — Responsible:** The person(s) who do the actual work
> - **A — Accountable:** The one person who owns the final outcome and signs off on it *(one person only per task)*
> - **C — Consulted:** Those whose input is sought before decisions are made
> - **I — Informed:** Those kept updated on progress and outcomes
>
> *(Source: RACI Matrix framework; ClearPoint Strategy — Ted Jackson, Harvard Business School alumnus, 30+ years in strategy execution)*

> ⚠️ Golden rule: Every task must have **exactly one A**. If more than one person is "ultimately accountable," no one actually is.
> *(Source: DecideAct — "RACI Matrix: Solve the People Problem in Strategy Execution"; HBR — Peter Bregman)*

## 9. Meeting & Review Cadence

**What to write:**
Define when and how you will review progress. Without a structured review rhythm, the plan dies in a drawer.

> 85% of leadership teams spend less than one hour per month discussing strategy. 49% of organizations have leaders who dedicate only one day per month to reviewing strategic implementation. This inattention is one of the primary drivers of execution failure.
> *(Source: JBOMS — "Why 90% of Business Plans Fail", 2025)*

| Meeting Type | Frequency | Participants | Duration | Required Output |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Task Stand-up** | Weekly | Execution team | 30 min | Task status update + blocker removal |
| **Operational Review** | Monthly | Managers + initiative owners | 60–90 min | KPI review + corrective decisions |
| **Strategic Review** | Quarterly | Senior leadership | Half day | Results vs. objectives + plan update |
| **Annual Strategy Review** | Yearly | Full leadership team | Full day | Strategy assessment + next year plan |

> 77% of successful companies have a formal, pre-established system to inform on and manage their strategy.
> *(Source: Palladium Group — cited in ClearPoint Strategy, 2025)*

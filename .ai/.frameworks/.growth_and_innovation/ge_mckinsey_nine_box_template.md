# GE-McKinsey Nine-Box Matrix — Portfolio Analysis Template

### McKinsey & Company × General Electric | Early 1970s

### First Application: GE's Portfolio of ~150 Business Units, 1970

### Research Sources: McKinsey.com · Umbrex · Hacking the Case Interview · SM Insight · UKEssays · MBA Crystal Ball · Cascade · ThinkInsights · The Strategy Institute · Deckary · ESADE · Wikipedia (2025–2026)

---

## Framework Overview

> _"The nine-box matrix offers a systematic approach for the decentralized corporation to determine where best to invest its cash. Rather than rely on each business unit's projections of its future prospects, the company can judge a unit by two factors that will determine whether it's going to do well in the future: the attractiveness of the relevant industry and the unit's competitive strength within that industry."_
> — McKinsey.com — Enduring Ideas: The GE–McKinsey Nine-Box Matrix

**Origin:**
In the 1970s, General Electric was managing a huge and complex portfolio of unrelated products and was unsatisfied with the returns from its investments. At the time, companies usually relied on projections of future cash flows, future market growth, or some other future projections to make investment decisions — an unreliable method. GE consulted McKinsey & Company, and as a result the nine-box framework was designed.

GE needed a more nuanced portfolio tool than the then-popular BCG Growth-Share Matrix to manage a large, diversified set of businesses. The framework was created to solve a practical problem: how to allocate capital and leadership attention across dozens of business units competing for resources, each facing different dynamics and performance profiles.

**Why nine boxes instead of four:**
McKinsey took inspiration from the BCG matrix — a 2×2 grid using just market growth and market share — and expanded it into a 3×3 grid that incorporates multiple weighted factors for each axis. The result is a more nuanced and comprehensive view of where to invest, where to hold, and where to divest.

**The key advancement over BCG:**
The GE-McKinsey matrix is the most rigorous portfolio strategy framework available to corporate strategists. Its value comes from forcing multi-factor evaluation where simpler tools rely on single metrics.

---

## BCG Matrix vs. GE-McKinsey — Why Upgrade?

| Dimension            | BCG Growth-Share Matrix                    | GE-McKinsey Nine-Box                              |
| -------------------- | ------------------------------------------ | ------------------------------------------------- |
| **Axes**             | Market growth rate + Relative market share | Industry attractiveness + Competitive strength    |
| **Factors per axis** | 1 each (single metric)                     | 5–10 weighted factors each                        |
| **Grid size**        | 2×2 = 4 cells                              | 3×3 = 9 cells                                     |
| **Nuance**           | Binary (high/low)                          | Three levels (high/medium/low)                    |
| **Best for**         | Initial portfolio screening                | Deep multi-business portfolio decisions           |
| **Weakness**         | Oversimplifies complex portfolios          | Scoring subjectivity; weight selection challenges |

> Start with the BCG matrix for initial portfolio screening. Graduate to the GE-McKinsey matrix when business units operate in fundamentally different industries or when competitive advantage depends on factors beyond market share.

```
  High      │  INVEST/GROW    │  INVEST/GROW │  SELECTIVITY │

  Medium    │  ④ Build        │  ⑤ Selective │  ⑥ Limited  │
(Y-AXIS)    │  Selectively    │  Investment/ │  Expansion   │
            │                 │  Manage for  │  or Harvest  │
            ├─────────────────┤  Earnings    ├──────────────┤
  Low       │  SELECTIVITY    │              │ HARVEST/     │
            │                 │  ⑧ Manage   │ DIVEST       │
            │  ⑦ Protect &   │  for         │              │
            │  Refocus        │  Earnings    │  ⑨ Divest   │
            └─────────────────┴──────────────┴──────────────┘

    🟢 INVEST/GROW zone (cells 1, 2, 4)     = Upper-left triangle
    🟡 SELECTIVITY zone (cells 3, 5, 7)     = Diagonal
    🔴 HARVEST/DIVEST zone (cells 6, 8, 9)  = Lower-right triangle
```

> Units above the diagonal may warrant investment and growth strategies, those on the diagonal selective investment, and those below the diagonal are candidates for sale, liquidation, or running for cash.

## Step 2 — Score Industry Attractiveness

**What it measures:**
Industry attractiveness indicates how hard or easy it will be for a company to compete in the market and earn profits. Industry attractiveness consists of many factors that collectively determine the competition level.

> **Critical instruction:** When evaluating industry attractiveness, look at long-term conditions rather than short-term trends. The investments required for a business unit typically demand a multi-year commitment, so a market that looks temporarily attractive may not justify a five-year capital commitment.

> **Important:** There is no definitive list of which factors should be included. Each company should choose and weight factors that are most relevant to its industry and situation.

---

### Industry Attractiveness — Factor Menu

> _(Source: SM Insight; UKEssays; Hacking the Case Interview; ESADE — 2025–2026)_

| Factor Category             | Factors to Consider                                                                                      |
| --------------------------- | -------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Market structure**        | Market size, market growth rate, market profitability, stage of industry lifecycle                       |
| **Competitive dynamics**    | Number of competitors, competitive intensity, concentration ratio, rivalry level                         |
| **Profitability drivers**   | Average industry margins, pricing power, cost structure                                                  |
| **Entry/Exit barriers**     | Capital requirements, regulatory barriers, switching costs, economies of scale                           |
| **Supplier/Buyer power**    | Supplier concentration, buyer concentration, bargaining power on both sides _(use Porter's Five Forces)_ |
| **Technology & innovation** | Rate of technological change, IP landscape, innovation requirements                                      |
| **Macro forces**            | Regulatory environment, political stability, macroeconomic conditions, ESG requirements                  |
| **Structural trends**       | Seasonality, cyclicality, degree of product differentiation                                              |

---

### Industry Attractiveness Scoring Table

**Instructions:**

1. Select 5–8 factors most relevant to your industry
2. Assign a weight to each factor (all weights must sum to 1.0)
3. Rate each factor for each BU on a scale of 1–5 (1 = very unattractive, 5 = very attractive)
4. Calculate: Weighted Score = Weight × Rating
5. Sum all weighted scores → Total Industry Attractiveness Score (max = 5.0)

> Weighted score = weight of the factor × rate for the unit. Total score = sum of all weighted scores for each business unit.

## PART 3 — AXIS 2: COMPETITIVE STRENGTH SCORING

---

## Step 3 — Score Competitive Strength

**What it measures:**
Competitive strength measures how strong the business unit is relative to competitors in its specific market. It is entirely about internal factors the company controls.

> **Critical instruction:** Score competitive strength **relative to competitors** — not in absolute terms. A score of 4/5 means you are strong _compared to the key rivals in that specific market_, not that the unit is strong in isolation.
>
> Independent scoring and relative-to-competitor calibration prevent the grid from becoming a self-congratulation exercise.

---

### Competitive Strength — Factor Menu

> _(Source: SM Insight; Cascade; UKEssays; Hacking the Case Interview — 2025–2026)_

| Factor Category           | Factors to Consider                                                    |
| ------------------------- | ---------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Market position**       | Market share, relative market share, market share trend                |
| **Financial strength**    | Profitability relative to competitors, cost structure, margin trend    |
| **Brand & reputation**    | Brand equity, customer loyalty, NPS, perceived quality                 |
| **Capabilities**          | R&D capability, innovation output, production efficiency, technology   |
| **Strategic assets**      | Patents, proprietary technology, licenses, exclusive supply agreements |
| **Competitive advantage** | VRIO resources (use VRIO Framework), sustainable differentiators       |
| **Operational**           | Supply chain strength, distribution network, customer service quality  |
| **People**                | Management quality, talent depth, culture and execution capability     |

---

### Competitive Strength Scoring Table

**Same methodology as Industry Attractiveness:**
Weight × Rating for each factor → Sum = Total Competitive Strength Score (max = 5.0)

> Rating qualitative elements necessarily relies considerably on individual judgment. This brings risks of personal bias, halo effects, recency bias, and scale perception issues. An overestimation of competitive strength based on a recent product success can skew business unit positioning considerably.

**Anti-bias measures:**

- Score each BU against specific competitor data, not internal perception
- Use the same analysts for all BUs to ensure consistency
- Require evidence for every rating above 4 or below 2
- Have an external reviewer challenge scores above 3.5

## PART 5 — THE THREE STRATEGIC ZONES

---

## Zone 1 — 🟢 INVEST / GROW

### Cells ①, ②, ④ — High-High, High-Medium, Medium-High

**What it means:**
The best position for a business to be in is the Invest/Grow section. A business can reach this scenario if it is operating in a moderate to highly attractive industry while having a moderate to highly competitive position within that industry.

These are the portfolio's crown jewels and high-potential engines. They deserve the highest priority for capital allocation, management attention, and talent deployment.

---

### Cell-by-Cell Strategy — Invest/Grow Zone

**Cell ① — High Attractiveness + Strong Competitive Position**
_"Protect & Build"_

> High Attractiveness / Strong Position (Invest to Grow): Allocate capital for share capture, capability building, and selective M&A; accept near-term ROI dilution with milestone gating.

Strategic priorities:

- Invest aggressively to widen the competitive lead
- Defend against challengers seeking to displace you
- Invest in capability building to sustain advantage as competition intensifies
- Consider selective M&A to accelerate scale

---

**Cell ② — High Attractiveness + Medium Competitive Position**
_"Invest to Build"_

> High Attractiveness / Medium Position (Selective Investment): Focus on advantaged niches; build/buy critical capabilities; partner where needed; avoid broad share wars.

Strategic priorities:

- Identify the specific capabilities or assets that would move you to a Strong position
- Focus investment on the 2–3 most defensible segments within the market
- Determine if organic investment or acquisition is the faster path to competitive leadership

---

**Cell ④ — Medium Attractiveness + Strong Competitive Position**
_"Build Selectively"_

Strategic priorities:

- Defend the strong competitive position — it is your primary asset in a moderately attractive market
- Invest in segments with the highest profitability, not necessarily the highest growth
- Resist over-investing in market growth beyond what your competitive strength can profitably exploit
- Look for ways to improve industry attractiveness through market development or innovation

## Zone 2 — 🟡 SELECTIVITY / EARNINGS

### Cells ③, ⑤, ⑦ — The Diagonal

**What it means:**
Those on the diagonal receive selective investment. These units require careful case-by-case analysis. They are neither clear investment priorities nor clear divestiture candidates. Management judgment — not formula — determines the right move.

---

### Cell-by-Cell Strategy — Selectivity Zone

**Cell ③ — High Attractiveness + Weak Competitive Position**
_"Build Selectively or Double-Down"_

> High Attractiveness / Weak Position (Double-or-Quit): If a credible path to strength exists (through acquisition or proprietary capability), commit meaningfully; otherwise, avoid incremental spend.

> This is the most interesting decision. The industry is highly attractive, but the division is competitively weak. Can we build strength through acquisition or R&D, or is the gap too large? This is where the GE-McKinsey matrix earns its complexity premium: the BCG matrix would classify this as a Question Mark, which is correct but unhelpful. The nine-box grid shows the gap is in competitive strength specifically, implying capability building is the right investment type.

Strategic priorities:

- Honestly assess: is there a credible, time-bounded path to competitive strength?
- If yes: invest significantly, with clear milestones and a decision gate
- If no: begin orderly exit before further value is destroyed in an attractive market you cannot win

---

**Cell ⑤ — Medium Attractiveness + Medium Competitive Position**
_"Selective Investment / Manage for Earnings"_

Strategic priorities:

- Identify the specific segments within this market where your competitive position is strongest
- Focus on profitability over growth — do not chase volume in a moderately attractive market
- Explore whether targeted investment could move this unit to Cell ① or ④
- Consider whether this unit is better owned by a different player — divestiture at value may be wiser than continued mediocrity

---

**Cell ⑦ — Low Attractiveness + Strong Competitive Position**
_"Protect & Refocus"_

Strategic priorities:

- Do not invest for market growth — the industry is unattractive
- Harvest the strong competitive position for cash generation
- Explore whether the strong competitive capability could be redeployed in a more attractive industry
- Watch for industry attractiveness improving — if external conditions change, reassess

## Zone 3 — 🔴 HARVEST / DIVEST

### Cells ⑥, ⑧, ⑨ — Weak Position or Low Attractiveness

**What it means:**
Those below the diagonal are candidates for sale, liquidation, or running for cash.

These units are consuming resources — capital, management time, organizational complexity — in situations where neither the industry nor the competitive position justifies continued investment.

---

### Cell-by-Cell Strategy — Harvest/Divest Zone

**Cell ⑥ — Medium Attractiveness + Weak Competitive Position**
_"Limited Expansion or Harvest"_

Strategic priorities:

- Assess whether a niche sub-segment exists where competitive weakness could be overcome
- If no credible path: begin systematic cash extraction; no new investment
- Identify potential acquirers for whom this unit would be strategically stronger

---

**Cell ⑧ — Low Attractiveness + Medium Competitive Position**
_"Manage for Earnings"_

Strategic priorities:

- Maximize cash generation within current operations — no growth investment
- Reduce cost structure to sustain acceptable margins
- Plan for eventual exit as industry attractiveness continues to erode
- Set a clear time horizon for the harvest period

---

**Cell ⑨ — Low Attractiveness + Weak Competitive Position**
_"Divest"_

> The buyer might have better competences to make it a success, or they can create value by combining activities (synergies). The cash that results from selling the business unit can consequently be used in Invest/Grow business units elsewhere in the portfolio.

Strategic priorities:

- Move quickly — value deteriorates as both industry and competitive position weaken further
- Find the most strategic buyer; maximize sale value
- Redeploy capital and management attention immediately to Invest/Grow zone units
- Do not allow sentimentality or organizational inertia to delay exit decisions

## Worked Example — Healthcare Conglomerate

> _(Adapted from Deckary — GE-McKinsey Matrix Guide, February 2026)_

| Division                    | Industry Attractiveness              | Competitive Strength                           | Cell | Zone              | Recommended Strategy                                                     |

| **Pharmaceutical Research** | High (growing demand, high margins)  | Medium (strong pipeline but not market leader) | ②    | 🟢 Invest/Grow    | Invest to Build — consider M&A to accelerate to Cell ①                   |
| **Healthcare Diagnostics**  | High (digital health growth)         | Weak (late entrant, low share)                 | ③    | 🟡 Selectivity    | Double-or-Quit — assess acquisition target to build competitive strength |
| **Hospital Supplies**       | Medium (mature market, stable)       | Strong (dominant supplier)                     | ④    | 🟢 Invest/Grow    | Build Selectively — harvest cash; invest in efficiency                   |

## What the GE-McKinsey Matrix Cannot Do

> _(Sources: SM Insight; Umbrex; The Strategy Institute; Deckary — 2025–2026)_

| Limitation                        | Detail                                                                                                                                                                           | How to Mitigate                                                                                      |
| --------------------------------- | -------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- | ---------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Scoring subjectivity**          | Rating qualitative elements necessarily relies considerably on individual judgment. This brings risks of personal bias, halo effects, recency bias, and scale perception issues. | Use competitor data for every rating; require evidence for outlier scores; use independent reviewers |
| **Scoring inflation**             | Business unit leaders score their own divisions generously.                                                                                                                      | Separate the people scoring from the people being scored; use external benchmarks                    |
| **Weight selection subjectivity** | Different weight choices can move a BU from one zone to another. There is no objective method for setting weights.                                                               | Document weight rationale; run sensitivity analysis — what weight changes would shift a BU's zone?   |
| **Over-averaging**                | A "medium" score hides a mix of high- and low-attractiveness segments.                                                                                                           | Disaggregate composite scores; investigate the individual factors behind borderline ratings          |
| **Static snapshot**               | The matrix reflects current conditions. A fast-moving industry can shift attractiveness ratings within 12–18 months.                                                             | Refresh annually; add trend indicators (↑ ↓ →) to each score                                         |
| **No synergy analysis**           | The matrix treats each BU in isolation. Strategic synergies between units — shared distribution, shared R&D, shared brand — are invisible.                                       | Supplement with a synergy mapping exercise for BUs in the same zone                                  |
| **Ignores external dynamics**     | Industry attractiveness is scored but competitive forces are not analyzed structurally.                                                                                          | Combine with **Porter's Five Forces** for each BU's industry                                         |

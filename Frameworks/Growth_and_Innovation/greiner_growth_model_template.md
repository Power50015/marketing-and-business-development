## Framework Overview

> *"The future of an organization may be less determined by outside forces than it is by the organization's history."*
> — Larry E. Greiner, Harvard Business Review, 1972

**Origin:**
Larry E. Greiner, Professor at the University of Southern California's Marshall School of Business, developed the Greiner Growth Model and published it in the Harvard Business Review in 1972 under the title *"Evolution and Revolution as Organizations Grow."* In 1998, he revisited the model in a follow-up HBR article, validating most of his original concepts while adding a sixth phase around external alliances.

The model was built on the observation of many companies across industries, identifying a consistent pattern: organizations do not grow linearly. Instead, they alternate between **periods of calm evolution** and **periods of turbulent crisis**, with each crisis forcing a fundamental change in how the organization is managed.

**The five dimensions of the model:**
*(Source: Greiner 1972; SSRN Abstract)*

1. **Age of the organization** — problems and solutions change as the company ages
2. **Size of the organization** — coordination and communication problems intensify as headcount grows
3. **Stages of evolution** — prolonged periods of calm growth within a phase
4. **Stages of revolution** — turbulent periods of significant organizational change between phases
5. **Growth rate of the industry** — faster industries compress the time spent in each phase

**The core insight:**
Each phase of growth contains the seeds of its own crisis. The management practices that drive success in one phase create the very problems that trigger the next crisis. What works brilliantly at 10 employees becomes dysfunctional at 100, and unworkable at 1,000.

## PART 2 — THE SIX PHASES IN DETAIL

---

## Phase 1 — Growth Through Creativity
### Crisis: Leadership

**What characterizes this phase:**
In this pioneering phase, the company is young and relatively small. The organization is informal and the employees are very loyal. There is a flat organizational structure and the entrepreneur is externally oriented — investing in new clients and building the product.

The entire organization runs on the energy, vision, and decision-making of the founder. Communication is frequent and informal. Everyone knows everything. Processes are improvised rather than designed.

> *"Everyone knows everything and there is a lot of discussion and mutual information."*
> *(Source: Wikipedia — Larry E. Greiner)*

**Examples:**
- Apple in the garage (Jobs + Wozniak) — two people building hardware with no formal structure
- Amazon in 1994 — Bezos packing books himself in his garage
- Google in 1998 — Page and Brin running search experiments in a Stanford dorm

---

### The Leadership Crisis — How It Arrives

> Because the organization is growing (too fast) and is becoming more complex, the entrepreneur is no longer able to take stock of the situation and a leadership crisis arises. Coordination and internal control can no longer be carried out by one person. There is a need for an improved structure.
> *(Source: Toolshero — Greiner Growth Model)*

**Warning signs that the Leadership Crisis has arrived:**

| Warning Sign | Observable Symptom |
|---|---|
| Founder is the bottleneck | Every decision waits for one person |
| Communication breakdown | Information is lost because it is all informal |
| Inconsistent execution | Different people do the same task differently because nothing is documented |
| Founder discomfort | Founder is better at building than managing — and hates the management demands |
| Key talent leaving | Experienced hires leave because they have no authority to act |

---

### Phase 1 — Self-Assessment

| Characteristic | Strongly Present | Partially Present | Not Present |
|---|---|---|---|
| Flat structure — founder makes most decisions | ☐ | ☐ | ☐ |
| Informal communication — no formal processes | ☐ | ☐ | ☐ |

| Founder overwhelmed by management demands | ☐ | ☐ | ☐ |
| No formal management layer yet | ☐ | ☐ | ☐ |

**Resolution — What must happen to move to Phase 2:**
Appointment of a professional manager or strong operational leader who can install structure without destroying the creative culture. This is often emotionally difficult for founders.

> The resolution of this crisis requires the entrepreneur to either step back from management (hire a CEO/COO) or develop genuine management capability they currently lack.
> *(Source: B2BFrameworks — Greiner's Growth Model)*

---

## Phase 2 — Growth Through Direction
### Crisis: Autonomy

**What characterizes this phase:**
Formal procedures are installed. There are budgets and targets to be met. The company expands its offer and people are overloaded with work. The founder of the business is still active and is a puppet master pulling all the strings, even though middle managers have been appointed.

A strong or capable director takes the lead, and the organization begins to operate more professionally. Functions are established: HR, Finance, Operations. Reporting lines are clarified.

**Examples:**
- Apple under John Sculley (1983–1993) — Jobs pushed out; professional management installed to scale the Mac business
- Amazon ~2000–2004 — Jeff Wilke brought in to professionalize operations; Bezos still central but building management infrastructure

---

### The Autonomy Crisis — How It Arrives

Middle managers grow frustrated. They are experienced and capable but cannot act without approval from above. The directive leadership style that built Phase 2 success now prevents the organization from moving quickly enough as it grows.

> *"Delegation becomes a necessity but it hasn't yet become automatic. The founder of the business is still active and is a puppet master pulling all the strings."*
> *(Source: B2BFrameworks — Greiner's Growth Model)*

**Warning signs that the Autonomy Crisis has arrived:**

| Warning Sign | Observable Symptom |
|---|---|
| Talented managers leaving | Capable people frustrated by lack of authority |
| Slow decision-making | Everything escalates to senior leadership |
| Leader fatigue | The central leader is making too many operational decisions |
| Missed opportunities | Decisions that need speed are delayed waiting for approval |
| Low morale in middle management | "We know what to do — we just can't do it" |

---

### Phase 2 — Self-Assessment

| Characteristic | Strongly Present | Partially Present | Not Present |
|---|---|---|---|
| Formal structures and functional departments in place | ☐ | ☐ | ☐ |
| Budgets, targets, and reporting processes installed | ☐ | ☐ | ☐ |
| Middle managers hired but with limited authority | ☐ | ☐ | ☐ |
| Talented people frustrated by centralized control | ☐ | ☐ | ☐ |
| Directive leadership style still dominant | ☐ | ☐ | ☐ |

**Resolution — What must happen to move to Phase 3:**
Genuine delegation of authority to business unit or functional leaders. This requires trust — and often a significant shift in the founder or CEO's management style.

---

## Phase 3 — Growth Through Delegation
### Crisis: Control

**What characterizes this phase:**
The company has recruited middle managers and the founder is able to delegate. Divisions or business units are given profit and loss responsibility. Managers are motivated by bonuses and performance targets. Communication from the top is less frequent and more formal — by exception.

Growth accelerates as empowered managers act without waiting for permission. Innovation increases at the division level.

**Examples:**
- General Electric under Jack Welch (1981–2001) — radical decentralization; 13 business divisions given full P&L responsibility
- Amazon ~2002 — "two-pizza teams" — small, autonomous product teams with their own roadmaps

---

### The Control Crisis — How It Arrives

Too much delegation creates a new problem. Divisions begin to diverge. Each unit optimizes for its own results rather than the company's overall performance. Senior leadership loses visibility into what is happening across the organization. Mistakes are made that senior leadership only discovers too late.

> *"Sometimes there is too much delegation to the divisional managers."*
> *(Source: B2BFrameworks — Greiner's Growth Model)*

> Because delegation assumes that those on the front lines have the skills and competencies to take responsibility, this new system has its own set of issues. This isn't always the case, with many people elevated to managerial roles without the necessary training.
> *(Source: KeyModels — Greiner's Growth Model)*

**Warning signs that the Control Crisis has arrived:**

| Warning Sign | Observable Symptom |
|---|---|
| Divisions acting as "kingdoms" | Each unit has its own strategy, culture, and systems |
| Loss of company-wide coherence | Customers experience inconsistency across divisions |
| Financial surprises | Senior leadership discovers problems that should have been caught earlier |
| Lack of knowledge sharing | Innovation in one division never reaches others |
| Brand dilution | Different units represent the company differently to the market |

---

### Phase 3 — Self-Assessment

| Characteristic | Strongly Present | Partially Present | Not Present |
|---|---|---|---|
| Divisions or BUs with own P&L and high autonomy | ☐ | ☐ | ☐ |
| Top management feeling out of control | ☐ | ☐ | ☐ |
| Divisions optimizing locally at expense of the whole | ☐ | ☐ | ☐ |
| Knowledge and best practices not shared across units | ☐ | ☐ | ☐ |
| Inconsistency in customer experience across divisions | ☐ | ☐ | ☐ |

**Resolution — What must happen to move to Phase 4:**
Introduction of formal coordination mechanisms: planning systems, shared services, standardized processes, centrally allocated funds, and company-wide incentives — without removing the autonomy gained in Phase 3.

---

## Phase 4 — Growth Through Coordination
### Crisis: Red-Tape

**What characterizes this phase:**
Growth continues when formerly independent divisions become more coordinated — through product groups, service practices, or shared services. More centrally allocated resources. Incentives based on firm-wide success. Formal planning cycles. Standardized systems.

The organization becomes more efficient and consistent. Customers experience a more unified company. Reporting and governance improve.

**Examples:**
- GE under Welch's later years — moving from pure decentralization to coordinated portfolio management
- McKinsey's practice group model — coordinating independent country offices through global practices and knowledge management systems

---

### The Red-Tape Crisis — How It Arrives

The coordination systems that restore order in Phase 4 eventually become the problem. Rules multiply. Committees proliferate. Every decision requires multiple sign-offs. The organization becomes slower than the market it serves.

> *"The crisis of red tape arises when the organisation has become too bureaucratic and the rules have started to impede the growth of the organisation."*
> *(Source: MBAKnol — Greiner's Model)*

**Warning signs that the Red-Tape Crisis has arrived:**

| Warning Sign | Observable Symptom |
|---|---|
| Innovation is slow | New ideas require months of approval cycles |
| Talented people leaving for startups | "I can't get anything done here" |
| Process over purpose | People follow the rules even when the rules are wrong |
| Customer frustration | External partners and customers frustrated by organizational slowness |
| Managers spending more time in meetings than creating value | Coordination overhead exceeds coordination value |
| Staff complaining about bureaucracy | "There's too much red tape here" is a common phrase |

---

### Phase 4 — Self-Assessment

| Characteristic | Strongly Present | Partially Present | Not Present |
|---|---|---|---|
| Strong formal planning and reporting systems | ☐ | ☐ | ☐ |
| Central coordination functions (shared services, COEs) | ☐ | ☐ | ☐ |
| Growing complaints about process and bureaucracy | ☐ | ☐ | ☐ |
| Slow decision-making despite clear organizational structure | ☐ | ☐ | ☐ |
| Innovation suffering due to approval complexity | ☐ | ☐ | ☐ |

**Resolution — What must happen to move to Phase 5:**
Significant simplification of processes. Move toward team-based structures, cross-functional collaboration, and psychological safety. Reduce rules; increase trust. This is a cultural transformation, not just a structural one.

---

## Phase 5 — Growth Through Collaboration
### Crisis: Internal Growth

**What characterizes this phase:**
The organization simplifies and humanizes. Matrix structures are replaced by cross-functional teams. Bureaucracy is reduced. Social control replaces formal control. Information systems become real-time and company-wide. Education and self-management develop.

The focus shifts from coordination through systems to coordination through culture, trust, and shared purpose.

**Examples:**
- Apple under Steve Jobs post-2001 — cross-functional product development teams, radical simplification, "One Apple" culture
- Google's early culture (2004–2015) — 20% time, OKRs, flat team structures, psychological safety as operating principle

---

### The Internal Growth Crisis — How It Arrives

After years of intense collaboration, teams become fatigued. The organization reaches the limits of what it can accomplish through internal collaboration alone. The business environment demands capabilities, markets, or technologies that the organization simply does not have and cannot build quickly enough internally.

> *"Greiner also referred to this as a potential 'crisis of internal growth' where organizations might need to look for new avenues for external growth or renewal."*
> *(Source: SuperBusinessManager — The Greiner Curve)*

**Warning signs that the Internal Growth Crisis has arrived:**

| Warning Sign | Observable Symptom |
|---|---|
| Employee fatigue and burnout | "We are trying to do too much with the same people" |
| Growth plateauing | Revenue and innovation are hitting a ceiling |
| Capability gaps | Critical capabilities needed for next growth horizon don't exist internally |
| Market opportunities missed | Opportunities requiring speed or expertise beyond current capacity |
| Psychological saturation | Teams are collaborative but exhausted by constant change |

---

### Phase 5 — Self-Assessment

| Characteristic | Strongly Present | Partially Present | Not Present |
|---|---|---|---|
| Strong cross-functional team culture | ☐ | ☐ | ☐ |
| Bureaucracy significantly reduced | ☐ | ☐ | ☐ |
| High trust and psychological safety | ☐ | ☐ | ☐ |
| Growth hitting a ceiling despite strong internal culture | ☐ | ☐ | ☐ |
| Recognition that external capabilities or markets are needed | ☐ | ☐ | ☐ |

**Resolution — What must happen to move to Phase 6:**
Look externally. Form strategic alliances, joint ventures, or acquisitions. Bring in external expertise and market access. This requires developing new skills in partnership management and M&A integration.

---

## Phase 6 — Growth Through Alliances
### Crisis: Identity / Interdependence

*Added by Greiner in his 1998 HBR update.*

**What characterizes this phase:**
Growth continues via mergers, acquisitions, outsourcing, networks, joint ventures, and any other external solution. The organization builds an ecosystem of partners rather than trying to own all capabilities internally.

> *"Greiner's sixth and final phase notes that company growth can continue via mergers, acquisitions, outsourcing, networking, and any other external solution."*
> *(Source: FourWeekMBA — Greiner's Growth Model)*

**Examples:**
- Apple's alliance with Google (Google as default search on Safari) — Apple leveraging external capability rather than building its own search
- Google's Android ecosystem — partnering with Samsung, OEMs, and app developers rather than building all hardware internally
- Amazon's AWS marketplace — an ecosystem of thousands of technology partners building on Amazon's platform
- Microsoft's OpenAI investment — external alliance to access AI capability at the frontier

---

### The Identity / Interdependence Crisis — How It Arrives

As the organization becomes more dependent on external partners, a new threat emerges: loss of identity and strategic independence. Partners have their own interests. Culture becomes diluted. The organization's core purpose becomes unclear.

> *"Because an organization is more focused on alliances than its own core-business, there is a good chance that an identity crisis will present itself."*
> *(Source: Toolshero — Greiner Growth Model)*

**Warning signs that the Identity Crisis has arrived:**

| Warning Sign | Observable Symptom |
|---|---|
| Cultural dilution | Employees unsure what the company stands for post-merger |
| Strategic drift | Partners' agendas pulling the organization in conflicting directions |
| Leadership succession challenges | Alliance complexity makes internal leadership development harder |
| Core business neglect | So much attention on alliances that the core business suffers |
| Brand confusion | Customers and employees unclear on what differentiates the organization from its partners |

---

### Phase 6 — Self-Assessment

| Characteristic | Strongly Present | Partially Present | Not Present |
|---|---|---|---|

| Leadership succession complexity growing | ☐ | ☐ | ☐ |

## Apple Inc. — Complete Greiner Journey

| Phase | Time Period | What Happened | Crisis That Triggered Transition |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Phase 1 — Creativity** | 1976–1983 | Jobs and Wozniak build Apple I and II in a garage; informal, founder-driven, explosive creativity | Leadership crisis — Apple growing too fast for founder-led management |
| **Phase 2 — Direction** | 1983–1993 | John Sculley brought in as CEO; professional management installed; Mac launch; structured marketing | Autonomy crisis — talented engineers and managers frustrated by top-down control; Jobs fired 1985 |
| **Phase 3 — Delegation** | 1993–1997 | Multiple CEOs attempt delegation; product lines multiply chaotically; divisions operating independently | Control crisis — product portfolio spiraling out of control; near-bankruptcy |
| **Phase 4 — Coordination** | 1997–2007 | Jobs returns; radical product simplification; "One Apple"; tight coordination through Jobs himself and small leadership team | Red-tape beginning; but Jobs' unique style kept coordination from becoming bureaucracy |
| **Phase 5 — Collaboration** | 2007–2015 | iPhone launch; cross-functional product teams; iOS ecosystem; Jony Ive + Tim Cook collaboration | Internal growth crisis — scale of global operations exceeds what collaboration alone can manage |
| **Phase 6 — Alliances** | 2015–present | Google as default search; Beats acquisition; OpenAI rumored; developer ecosystem; manufacturing alliances | Identity/Interdependence crisis — maintaining Apple's differentiation while deeply dependent on Google and others |

## PART 7 — LIMITATIONS & CRITICAL PERSPECTIVE

---

## What the Greiner Model Cannot Do

> *(Sources: Studocu — Amazon Greiner Analysis; SuperBusinessManager; Lucidity — 2025–2026)*

| Limitation | Detail | How to Mitigate |
|---|---|---|
| **Too linear and sequential** | Real organizational growth is not always orderly. Companies can skip phases, regress under pressure, or experience multiple crises simultaneously. | Use the model as a directional guide, not a rigid sequence. Assess which crisis symptoms are most dominant. |
| **Ignores external factors** | The model does not account for market disruptions, recessions, regulatory changes, or competitive shocks that can force a transition regardless of the organization's internal readiness. | Combine with **PESTLE** and **Porter's Five Forces** for external context |
| **Timing is unpredictable** | The model identifies *what* will happen but not *when*. A company can stay in Phase 2 for 2 years or 20 years depending on industry growth rate, founder style, and market conditions. | Use the industry growth rate dimension — faster industries compress phases |
| **Not applicable to all organization types** | The model was built on observations of traditional hierarchical organizations. Platform businesses, DAOs, and distributed organizations may follow fundamentally different growth dynamics. | Apply with judgment; adapt to organizational type |
| **Prescriptive in non-linear environments** | The solutions prescribed for each crisis may not apply in today's fast-moving digital environments where agility is constant. | Treat prescribed solutions as options, not mandates |
| **No financial framework** | The model does not link growth phases to financial metrics, investment requirements, or cash flow implications. | Combine with **BCG Matrix** or **GE-McKinsey Matrix** for financial portfolio perspective |

> *"A business such as Amazon should be wary about relying on Greiner's model of growth as the model is criticised for being too simplistic. Real growth isn't as smooth as this or always in order."*
> *(Source: Studocu — Greiner's Growth Model Analysis)*

| Purpose | Framework to Use Alongside |

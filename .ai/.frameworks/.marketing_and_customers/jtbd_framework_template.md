## Before You Start — Critical Instructions

> **The single most important rule of JTBD:**
> _"People don't want a quarter-inch drill. They want a quarter-inch hole." — Theodore Levitt, Harvard Business School. But even Levitt didn't go far enough. They don't want a hole either. They want to hang a picture. They want to make their home feel complete. JTBD asks you to see the whole story — not just the functional task, but the emotional and social progress the customer is trying to make._
> _(Source: Clayton Christensen — "Competing Against Luck", 2016; SUE Behavioural Design — JTBD Applied, 2025)_

**How to use this template:**

**Step 1** — Define the **Main Job** your customers are hiring your product/service to do — using the Job Statement format.
**Step 2** — Map the **Three Dimensions** (Functional, Emotional, Social) of the job.
**Step 3** — Complete the **Forces of Progress Diagram** to understand what drives customers toward your solution and what holds them back.
**Step 4** — Build the **Job Map** — the universal steps customers go through to get the job done.
**Step 5** — Identify **underserved outcomes** — where customers are not satisfied with current solutions.
**Step 6** — Use findings to drive product, marketing, and strategy decisions.

> ⚠️ **Research warning:** The #1 mistake when applying JTBD is defining the job in terms of your product ("help customers use our software") instead of the customer's desired progress ("help customers organize their finances to feel in control"). The job must be **solution-agnostic** — it existed before your product and will exist after it.
> _(Source: Strategyn — ODI Methodology, 2025; AriseGTM — JTBD Framework Guide, 2025)_

> 🔁 **Review cycle:** Jobs are stable over long periods — they don't change with technology. But the _context_ and _competing solutions_ change constantly. Revisit your Forces of Progress and competitive landscape **every 6 months**.
> _(Source: Strategyn — Tony Ulwick, "Jobs are stable, solutions change", 2025)_

## Core Concepts — Quick Reference

| Concept                | Definition                                                                           | Why It Matters                                                                       |
| ---------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------ | ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------ |
| **The Job**            | The progress a customer is trying to make in a specific circumstance                 | You are not selling a product — you are enabling progress                            |
| **Hiring & Firing**    | Customers "hire" products to do jobs and "fire" them when they fail                  | Your competition is whatever the customer was doing before — including doing nothing |
| **Functional Job**     | The practical, tangible task to be accomplished                                      | The most visible part of the job — but rarely the whole story                        |
| **Emotional Job**      | How the customer wants to feel during and after the job                              | Often the hidden driver behind purchase decisions                                    |
| **Social Job**         | How the customer wants to be perceived by others                                     | Status, belonging, competence — the social layer of every choice                     |
| **Forces of Progress** | Push, Pull, Anxiety, and Inertia — the four forces that determine switching behavior | If Push + Pull > Anxiety + Inertia, the customer switches                            |
| **Desired Outcomes**   | The metrics customers use to measure how well a job is getting done                  | The basis for Outcome-Driven Innovation (ODI)                                        |
| **Job Map**            | The 8-step universal process for getting any job done                                | Reveals where current solutions fail — regardless of technology                      |

_(Source: Christensen Institute — JTBD Theory, 2025; Strategyn — ODI, 2025; JTBD.info — Forces of Progress, 2025)_

## 1. The Main Job Statement

**What it means:**
The core job your customers are trying to get done. This must be stated in terms of the _customer's desired progress_ — not your product's features. A good job statement is stable, solution-agnostic, and actionable.

> **Job Statement Format:**
> _"When [circumstance / trigger], I want to [motivation / the job], so I can [desired outcome / progress]."_
> _(Source: James Mattison — JTBD Job Statement Format, 2025; UserInterviews — Job Story Template, 2025)_

**Common mistake:**
Defining the job too narrowly (tied to your product) or too broadly (meaningless). "Manage my finances" is too broad. "Use your budgeting app" is too narrow. "Track my spending in real-time so I can feel confident that I'm living within my means" is a good job statement.

### Diagnostic Questions

- Are you addressing the functional job only — or have you also designed for the emotional and social dimensions?
  _(Source: Christensen Institute — "Jobs have functional, emotional, and social dimensions", 2025)_
- Which dimension drives the purchase decision most? (Often emotional, not functional.)
- If a competitor matched all your functional features, would customers still choose you? If yes, why? (The answer reveals your emotional/social advantage.)
- Are your marketing messages speaking to the functional task — or to the progress and feelings the customer actually cares about?
  _(Source: TheyDo — JTBD in Marketing, 2025)_

## 3. Forces of Progress Diagram

**What it means:**
The four forces that determine whether a customer will switch from their current solution to yours. Understanding these forces explains not just _what_ customers want, but _why they do (or don't) take action_.

> _"Switching happens when the combined strength of Push and Pull exceeds the combined resistance of Anxiety and Inertia."_
> _(Source: JTBD.info — Forces of Progress, Bob Moesta, 2025; SpatialRD — Forces Diagram, 2025)_

**Net force calculation:**

- Push + Pull = **\_** (Drive to switch)
- Anxiety + Inertia = **\_** (Resistance to switch)
- **Net:** If Drive > Resistance → customer will switch

**Strategic implications:**

- To increase switching: increase Push awareness (remind them of their pain) and Pull attractiveness (showcase the better outcome)
- To reduce resistance: reduce Anxiety (offer trials, guarantees, social proof) and Inertia (make migration easy, reduce switching costs)

_(Source: JTBD.info — Bob Moesta, Forces of Progress, 2025; Tempo.io — JTBD Forces Analysis, 2025)_

- What is the customer's current workaround? (The answer reveals both the job and the competitive set.)

## 4. The Job Map

**What it means:**
A structured decomposition of the job into eight universal steps. Every job — regardless of industry or technology — follows this sequence. The Job Map reveals where current solutions fail, creating opportunities for innovation.

> _"The Job Map is not a customer journey map. A customer journey is specific to a particular product or service. The Job Map is solution-agnostic — it maps the universal steps of getting the job done, regardless of how."_
> _(Source: Strategyn — Job Map Methodology, 2025; Jobs-to-be-done.com — Job Mapping, 2025)_

---

### The 8 Steps of the Job Map

| Step                                           | Definition                                                            | Your Customer's Experience | Pain Points / Unmet Needs |
| ---------------------------------------------- | --------------------------------------------------------------------- | -------------------------- | ------------------------- |
| **1. Define** — What needs to be accomplished? | Determine the goals, plan the approach, assess resources needed       |                            |                           |
| **2. Locate** — Gather what's needed           | Find and gather the inputs, information, or materials required        |                            |                           |
| **3. Prepare** — Set up for execution          | Organize and prepare the environment, inputs, or components           |                            |                           |
| **4. Confirm** — Verify readiness              | Validate that everything is ready and conditions are right to proceed |                            |                           |
| **5. Execute** — Perform the core task         | Carry out the main activity — the heart of the job                    |                            |                           |
| **6. Monitor** — Track progress                | Assess whether the job is being done successfully, in real-time       |                            |                           |
| **7. Modify** — Make adjustments               | Make changes or corrections if something isn't working as expected    |                            |                           |
| **8. Conclude** — Finish and follow up         | Wrap up, clean up, and assess the final result                        |                            |                           |

_(Source: Strategyn — Tony Ulwick, Job Map Framework, 2025; Jobs-to-be-done.com — Universal Job Map, 2025; SivoInsights — Job Mapping Guide, 2025)_

## 5. Desired Outcomes (ODI)

**What it means:**
Desired outcomes are the specific metrics customers use — consciously or unconsciously — to judge how well a job is getting done. Tony Ulwick's Outcome-Driven Innovation (ODI) methodology uses these outcomes to identify underserved needs with mathematical precision.

> **Outcome Statement Format:**
> _"Minimize the [time / likelihood / effort] it takes to [desired outcome] when [circumstance]."_
> _(Source: Strategyn — ODI Outcome Statements, 2025; AnthonyUlwick.com — Desired Outcomes, 2025)_

---

### Outcome Statements

| #   | Outcome Statement                  | Importance (1–10) | Satisfaction (1–10) | Opportunity Score |
| --- | ---------------------------------- | ----------------- | ------------------- | ----------------- |
| 1   | Minimize the time it takes to...   |                   |                     |                   |
| 2   | Minimize the likelihood of...      |                   |                     |                   |
| 3   | Minimize the effort required to... |                   |                     |                   |
| 4   |                                    |                   |                     |                   |
| 5   |                                    |                   |                     |                   |
| 6   |                                    |                   |                     |                   |

> **Opportunity Score** = Importance + max(Importance − Satisfaction, 0)
> Scores above 10 = **underserved** (high importance, low satisfaction) → prime innovation opportunities
> Scores below 6 = **overserved** (low importance, high satisfaction) → potential for cost reduction
> _(Source: Strategyn — ODI Opportunity Algorithm, 2025; Digital Leadership — JTBD Quantification, 2025)_

## 6. Competitive Analysis — Through the JTBD Lens

**What it means:**
In JTBD, your competition is not just companies selling similar products. Your competition is _every solution the customer currently uses or considers_ to get the same job done — including doing nothing, using a workaround, or hiring a completely different type of product.

> _"Customers don't compare products within categories. They compare solutions across categories — anything that gets the job done."_
> _(Source: Christensen — "Competing Against Luck", 2016)_

## 7. JTBD Interview Guide

**Instructions:** Use these questions in customer discovery interviews. The goal is to uncover the _story behind the switch_ — not to validate your product.

### The Timeline Interview (Switch Story)

| Phase               | Questions                                                                                         |
| ------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **First Thought**   | "When did you first start thinking about looking for a solution? What was happening?"             |
| **Passive Looking** | "What did you start noticing or researching? Were you actively searching or just becoming aware?" |
| **Active Looking**  | "What triggered you to start actively evaluating options? What was the tipping point?"            |
| **Decision**        | "What made you choose this specific solution? What was the deciding factor?"                      |
| **Post-Purchase**   | "After you started using it, what surprised you? What was better or worse than expected?"         |

_(Source: dScout — JTBD Switch Interview Methodology, 2025; JTBD.info — Bob Moesta Interview Framework, 2025)_

### Forces Discovery Questions

| Force       | Questions                                                                                                                       |
| ----------- | ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Push**    | "What about your old situation was becoming unbearable?" / "What was the specific frustration or struggle?"                     |
| **Pull**    | "What were you hoping the new solution would do for you?" / "What attracted you most?"                                          |
| **Anxiety** | "What were you worried might go wrong if you switched?" / "What concerns did you have?"                                         |
| **Inertia** | "Was there anything about your old way of doing things that you were afraid to lose?" / "What kept you from switching earlier?" |

_(Source: JTBD.info — Forces Interview Questions, Bob Moesta, 2025; Koji.so — JTBD Interview Guide, 2025)_

## 9. When to Use This Framework

| Situation                        | How JTBD Helps                                                                                |
| -------------------------------- | --------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| New product development          | Ensures the product solves a real job — not an assumed problem                                |
| Product-market fit assessment    | Validates whether your solution matches the customer's job, not just their stated preferences |
| Competitive strategy             | Reveals the real competitive set (including non-obvious competitors and workarounds)          |
| Marketing messaging              | Shifts messaging from features to the progress customers want to make                         |
| Pricing strategy                 | Helps price based on the value of the job being done, not cost-plus or competitive matching   |
| Innovation pipeline              | Identifies underserved outcomes as systematic innovation opportunities (ODI)                  |
| Customer research and interviews | Provides a structured methodology for understanding why customers switch (or don't)           |

_(Source: Aha.io — JTBD Applications, 2025; Strategyn — ODI Use Cases, 2025)_

## 10. Framework Limitations — Know What It Cannot Do

| Limitation                                                                   | What to Use Instead                                                     |
| ---------------------------------------------------------------------------- | ----------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| Does not address pricing strategy in detail                                  | Combine with **Value Proposition Canvas** and pricing frameworks        |
| Does not map the full customer experience with your specific product         | Use **Customer Journey Map** for touchpoint-level analysis              |
| Requires deep qualitative research — cannot be done from a conference room   | Invest in JTBD interviews with 10–15+ recent switchers                  |
| Does not address operational execution or marketing channels                 | Combine with **7Ps Marketing Mix** and go-to-market strategy            |
| Can be over-abstracted — "the job" becomes so high-level it's not actionable | Keep jobs specific enough to drive product and marketing decisions      |
| Outcome-Driven Innovation (ODI) quantification requires survey data at scale | Start with qualitative interviews, then scale with quantitative surveys |

> _"JTBD is a lens, not a process. It changes how you see the customer — but you still need execution frameworks to act on what you see."_
> _(Source: Dumbo.design — JTBD Framework Limitations, 2025)_

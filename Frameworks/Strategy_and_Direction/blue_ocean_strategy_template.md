---

## Framework Overview

> *"In red oceans, demand is created rather than fought over. There is ample opportunity for growth that is both profitable and rapid. In red oceans — that is, in all the industries already existing — companies compete by grabbing for a greater share of limited demand. As the market space gets more crowded, prospects for profits and growth decline. Products turn into commodities, and increasing competition turns the water bloody."*
> — Kim & Mauborgne, Harvard Business Review, October 2004

**The core idea:**
Blue Ocean Strategy (BOS), developed by W. Chan Kim and Renée Mauborgne at INSEAD, is built on a single powerful insight: lasting success comes not from fighting competitors, but from creating **uncontested market space** that makes competition irrelevant. It was based on a study of **150 strategic moves spanning more than 100 years and 30 industries**.
*(Source: SlideShare — Blue Ocean Strategy Summary; UKEssays, October 2025)*

**The central innovation — Value Innovation:**
BOS rejects Porter's assumption that differentiation and low cost are mutually exclusive trade-offs. Instead, it argues that companies can pursue **both simultaneously** through what Kim & Mauborgne call *Value Innovation* — innovating in ways that create a leap in value for buyers while simultaneously reducing or eliminating costs by dropping what buyers don't value.
*(Source: UKEssays — Blue Ocean Strategy Guide, October 2025)*

---

## Red Ocean vs. Blue Ocean — The Fundamental Contrast

| Dimension | 🔴 Red Ocean Strategy | 🔵 Blue Ocean Strategy |
|---|---|---|
| **Market space** | Compete in existing markets | Create uncontested market space |
| **Competition** | Beat the competition | Make the competition irrelevant |
| **Demand** | Exploit existing demand | Create and capture new demand |
| **Value-cost trade-off** | Make the choice between value or cost | Break the value-cost trade-off |
| **Strategic alignment** | Align whole system with differentiation OR cost | Align whole system with differentiation AND cost |
| **Customer focus** | Serve existing customers better | Reach beyond existing customers to noncustomers |
| **Metric of success** | Market share | Market creation |

> *(Source: BlueOceanStrategy.com — Red Ocean vs. Blue Ocean Strategy; Kim & Mauborgne, 2005)*

## Tool 1 — Strategy Canvas (As-Is)

**What it is:**
The Strategy Canvas is the central diagnostic tool of BOS. It graphically captures, in one simple picture, the **current competitive landscape** — the factors the industry competes on, what players invest in, and what buyers receive.

> *"It captures the current state of play in the known market space, which allows users to clearly see the factors that an industry competes on and invests in, what buyers receive, and what the strategic profiles of the major players are."*
> *(Source: BlueOceanStrategy.com — Strategy Canvas)*

**How to build it:**
- **X-axis:** The competing factors in your industry (10–15 maximum; phrase as buyer outcomes, not internal activities)
- **Y-axis:** The level of offering each player provides on each factor (Low → High)
- **Value curve:** A line connecting each player's offering level across all factors

> **Critical rule:** Competing factors must be defined from the **buyer's perspective** — not from your internal activities or technology. Use customer interviews, win/loss analysis, and jobs-to-be-done research to identify what buyers actually compare.
> *(Source: Umbrex — Blue Ocean Strategy Canvas Explained, December 2025)*

## Tool 2 — The Four Actions Framework

**What it is:**
A set of four questions that challenge every assumption about *why* your industry competes on the factors it does — enabling you to reconstruct buyer value and your cost structure simultaneously.

> *"The four actions framework is used to reconstruct buyer value elements in crafting a new value curve or strategic profile."*
> *(Source: BlueOceanStrategy.com — Four Actions Framework)*

---

### The Four Questions

**Question 1 — ELIMINATE:**
*Which factors that the industry has long competed on should be eliminated?*

These are factors that have been competed on for so long that no one questions their value anymore. Buyers may no longer care about them — or they may actively create complexity and cost without adding utility.

> **The logic of the four questions:** Eliminate and Reduce drive **cost down**. Raise and Create drive **value up**. Done together, they break the value-cost trade-off — the core of Value Innovation.
> *(Source: UKEssays — Blue Ocean Strategy Guide, October 2025; Umbrex, December 2025)*

## Tool 3 — The ERRC Grid

**What it is:**
The ERRC (Eliminate–Reduce–Raise–Create) Grid operationalizes the Four Actions Framework into a simple one-page strategic map. It forces action on **all four** dimensions simultaneously — not just raising and creating (which is what most companies default to, which only adds cost).

> *"The ERRC grid pushes companies not only to ask the questions but also to act on all four to create a new value curve."*
> *(Source: BlueOceanStrategy.com — ERRC Grid)*

> **Common mistake:** Organizations naturally gravitate toward Raise and Create while ignoring Eliminate and Reduce. This results in higher costs without a true blue ocean — just an expensive red ocean improvement.
> *(Source: SM Insight — Blue Ocean Strategy in Action, June 2025)*

## Tool 7 — The Sequence of Creating a Blue Ocean

**What it is:**
Before committing to a blue ocean move, Kim & Mauborgne argue that companies must validate it in a specific order. Getting the sequence wrong leads to failure — most often by building something no one values or no one can afford.

> *"Companies need to build their blue ocean strategy in the sequence of buyer utility, price, cost, and adoption."*
> *(Source: BlueOceanStrategy.com — Sequence of Creating a Blue Ocean)*

---

### Step 1 — Buyer Utility Test

**Does the offering unlock exceptional utility for the buyer?**

> If your blue ocean idea doesn't deliver a genuine leap in value for the buyer, stop here. Refine the idea or choose a different opportunity.

| Question | Answer |
|---|---|
| Does it remove the biggest blocks to utility identified in the Buyer Utility Map? | ☐ Yes ☐ No ☐ Partially |
| Do buyers (including noncustomers) confirm the value through research? | ☐ Yes ☐ No ☐ Not yet tested |
| Is the leap in value obvious to the buyer — not just to us internally? | ☐ Yes ☐ No |

**Utility test result:** ☐ Pass — proceed | ☐ Fail — refine the offering first

---

### Step 2 — Price Test (Price Corridor of the Mass)

**Is the price accessible to the mass of target buyers — including noncustomers?**

> A blue ocean offering priced too high will fail to unlock new demand, even if it delivers exceptional value. The goal is to set a price that the broadest possible group of buyers can access.

| Question | Answer |
|---|---|
| What is the strategic price point that targets the mass of buyers? | |
| Is this price accessible to our Tier 1 and Tier 2 noncustomers? | ☐ Yes ☐ No — too high |
| Can we protect this price from imitation for long enough to build market leadership? | ☐ Yes ☐ No — too easy to copy |

**Price test result:** ☐ Pass — proceed | ☐ Fail — reconsider pricing or cost structure

---

### Step 3 — Cost Test

**Can we deliver the offering at the target cost with a profitable margin?**

> Value Innovation requires that the ERRC grid's Eliminate and Reduce decisions fund the Raise and Create investments. If the cost structure doesn't work, the blue ocean idea is not viable.

| Question | Answer |
|---|---|
| Do Eliminate + Reduce decisions cover the cost of Raise + Create investments? | ☐ Yes ☐ No ☐ Partially |
| Can we achieve the target cost through operational improvements, partnerships, or pricing innovation? | ☐ Yes ☐ No |
| Is the margin at the strategic price sufficient for a sustainable business? | ☐ Yes ☐ No |

**Cost test result:** ☐ Pass — proceed | ☐ Fail — revisit ERRC grid or consider strategic pricing

---

### Step 4 — Adoption Test (Overcome Hurdles)

**Have we addressed the key barriers to adoption?**

> Kim & Mauborgne identify **four hurdles** that block strategy execution. All four must be addressed before a blue ocean move can succeed.
> *(Source: Ivory Research — Blue Ocean Strategy and SMEs; BlueOceanStrategy.com)*

| Hurdle | Description | How We Will Address It |
|---|---|---|
| **Cognitive hurdle** | Employees and stakeholders don't see the need for change; they are comfortable in the current red ocean | |
| **Resource hurdle** | Limited resources to fund the shift to a new strategy | |
| **Motivational hurdle** | Key players lack motivation to act; resistance from those benefiting from the status quo | |
| **Political hurdle** | Internal and external opposition from powerful stakeholders who stand to lose from the change | |

**Adoption test result:** ☐ All hurdles addressable — proceed | ☐ One or more hurdles unresolved — develop mitigation plan

## Monitoring — When Does Your Blue Ocean Become Red?

> *"Fast imitation risk: if not strongly protected, successful blue oceans can quickly become red oceans."*
> *(Source: Industry Leaders Magazine — Blue Ocean vs. Red Ocean, July 2025)*

| Signal | What to Watch For | Response |
|---|---|---|
| Competitors entering your space | How many? How fast? | Revisit ERRC Grid; move the value curve |
| Margins beginning to compress | Price pressure re-emerging | Extend into adjacent blue oceans |
| Your value curve converging with competitors | Offerings looking similar again | Apply Six Paths again; find new noncustomer insights |
| Buyers treating your innovation as a commodity | Loss of WTP premium | Refresh Create and Raise factors |

**Review cadence:** Revisit the Strategy Canvas **annually** or after any significant competitive move.
*(Source: Toolshero — Blue Ocean Strategy, 2025; Umbrex, December 2025)*

| Company | Industry Entered | Key Blue Ocean Move | Noncustomers Reached |

## Framework Limitations & Critical Perspective

| Limitation | Detail |
|---|---|
| **High uncertainty and execution risk** | Innovating into unknown territory involves significant research, development, and potential failure. Blue ocean ideas built on assumptions rather than validated buyer research frequently fail. *(Industry Leaders Magazine, July 2025)* |
| **Fast imitation** | Successful blue oceans attract competitors rapidly. Without patents, network effects, or structural advantages, a blue ocean can become a red ocean within 2–5 years. |
| **Organizational resistance** | Employees and stakeholders accustomed to red ocean tactics often resist the cultural change required. The four execution hurdles must be actively managed. *(Ivory Research — BOS and SMEs)* |
| **Resource intensity** | Creating new market space requires significant upfront investment in research, product development, and market education. |
| **Not a substitute for competitive analysis** | BOS does not analyze existing competitive dynamics in depth. Combine with **Porter's Five Forces** to understand the red ocean you are leaving and the structural risks of the blue ocean you are entering. |
| **Still requires a generic strategy** | Finding a blue ocean does not exempt you from Porter's logic. Once you define the new space, you still need to decide whether you are competing on differentiation or cost — and design your activity system accordingly. *(Umbrex, December 2025)* |

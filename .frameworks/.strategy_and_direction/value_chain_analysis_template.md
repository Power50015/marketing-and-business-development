## Framework Overview

> _"A value chain is a set of activities that an organization carries out to create value for its customers. Porter proposed a general-purpose value chain that companies can use to examine all of their activities, and see how they're connected. The way in which value chain activities are performed determines costs and affects profits."_
> — MindTools — Porter's Value Chain, January 2026

**Origin:**
Michael Porter introduced Value Chain Analysis in his 1985 book _Competitive Advantage: Creating and Sustaining Superior Performance_ (Free Press, New York). It builds directly on his earlier Five Forces framework (1979) by shifting focus from the industry level to the firm level — examining exactly _how_ a company creates value internally.
_(Source: IFM Cambridge — Porter's Value Chain; Porter, M.E., 1985, Ch. 1, pp. 11–15)_

**The core idea:**
Most organizations engage in hundreds, even thousands, of activities in the process of converting inputs to outputs. Value Chain Analysis disaggregates the firm into its strategically relevant activities in order to understand the behavior of costs and the existing and potential sources of competitive advantage.
_(Source: IFM Cambridge — Porter's Value Chain)_

**The two paths:**
Value Chain Analysis is conducted differently depending on your strategic objective:

- **Cost Advantage path** — find where costs are higher than they should be and reduce them
- **Differentiation Advantage path** — find where value can be enhanced in ways buyers will pay for

> _(Source: SM Insight — Value Chain Analysis: The Ultimate Guide; SlideShare — Value Chain Analysis for Competitive Advantage)_

## Critical Distinction — Value Chain vs. Supply Chain

> _(Source: Zendesk — Value Chain Analysis, 2025)_

|                      | **Value Chain**                                                                                               | **Supply Chain**                                                                               |
| -------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- | ---------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Scope**            | All activities contributing to a product's value: R&D, design, production, marketing, sales, customer service | Logistical flow of goods: movement and storage of materials, WIP inventory, and finished goods |
| **Focus**            | How value is created and where competitive advantage lives                                                    | How goods flow from origin to consumption                                                      |
| **Strategic use**    | Identifying cost and differentiation opportunities                                                            | Optimizing logistics and procurement efficiency                                                |
| **Porter's purpose** | Find where you outperform or underperform competitors                                                         | N/A — different tool                                                                           |

> When analyzing your value chain, it's important to figure out which competitive advantage is your main focus. This will help shape your efforts while enabling you to evaluate the effectiveness of your value chain analysis. Companies focused on differentiation typically invest more in support activities; those focused on cost leadership optimize primarily through primary activities.

## Primary Activity 1 — Inbound Logistics

**What it is:**
All activities required to receive, store, and disseminate inputs — the relationship between the firm and its suppliers. Involves raw material handling, warehousing, inventory control, vehicle scheduling, and returns to suppliers.
_(Source: IFM Cambridge — Porter's Value Chain, citing Porter 1985)_

**Real-world benchmark:**
Walmart's inbound logistics — particularly its proprietary distribution network and cross-docking system — was substantially better than Kmart's. This single activity advantage cascaded through all other primary activities and became the foundation of Walmart's cost leadership.
_(Source: StrategyU — Value Chain Analysis, March 2026)_

## Primary Activity 4 — Marketing & Sales

**What it is:**
Activities that enable buyers to purchase the product and that incentivize them to do so. Includes advertising, promotion, sales force, channel selection, pricing, and channel relationship management.
_(Source: IFM Cambridge — Porter's Value Chain, citing Porter 1985)_

**Real-world benchmark — Apple:**
Apple products' success comes not primarily from superior product features — other companies offer high-quality alternatives. It comes from Apple's exceptional marketing activities, which create perceived value that justifies a premium price in buyers' minds. Marketing IS the differentiation driver.
_(Source: SM Insight — Value Chain Analysis: The Ultimate Guide)_

**Real-world benchmark — Starbucks:**
Starbucks' marketing strategy — birthday rewards, Starbucks Rewards Card, social media promotion, in-store experience, baristas using customer first names — creates a powerful emotional connection that justifies its premium pricing. New products are heavily promoted in-store and online.
_(Source: Canva — Value Chain Analysis Guide, November 2025)_

## PART 2 — SUPPORT ACTIVITIES

**Definition:** The four activities that support the primary activities and each other by providing purchased inputs, technology, human resources, and various firm-wide functions. A support activity — such as HR management — creates value not just for one primary activity but across all of them.
_(Source: Toolshero — Porter's Value Chain Analysis, January 2026; The Strategy Institute)_

---

The organizational systems and functions that support the entire value chain — not just individual activities. Includes general management, planning, finance, accounting, legal, investor relations, quality management, and government affairs. Firm Infrastructure supports the margin of the _entire_ chain.

## Support Activity 3 — Technology Development

**What it is:**
Every value activity embeds technology — not just IT, but knowledge, procedures, and equipment. Technology Development includes R&D, product and process design, software development, automation, and technical know-how that improves how activities are performed.
_(Source: IFM Cambridge — Porter's Value Chain, citing Porter 1985)_

> Leveraging technological advancements is crucial for developing manufacturing techniques and automating processes, thereby enhancing efficiency and productivity across the value chain.

**Real-world benchmark — IKEA linkage:**
IKEA's Technology Development activity (flat-pack design) directly reduces Outbound Logistics costs (cheaper shipping), eliminates labor from Operations (no factory assembly), and shapes the Service activity (customer self-assembly). This is the power of linkages.

## The Most Important Insight: It's About the Linkages

> _"The ability to coordinate linkages often reduces cost or enhances differentiation. The distinction matters because competitors can often copy individual activities, but the linkages between them are harder to see and harder to replicate."_
> _(Source: StrategyU — Value Chain Analysis, March 2026)_

> _"Understanding the connections or linkages between activities can highlight areas for improvement."_
> _(Source: Boardmix — What is Value Chain Analysis, September 2024)_

**Why linkages matter more than individual activities:**
Individual activities are easy to benchmark and copy. The _system-level fit_ — how activities reinforce and enable each other — is what creates truly defensible competitive advantage.

## Framework Limitations — What Value Chain Analysis Cannot Do

> _(Source: Lucidity — Porter's Value Chain, May 2025; StrategyU, March 2026)_

| Limitation                                     | Detail                                                                                                                                                                        | What to Use Instead or Alongside                                                                                               |
| ---------------------------------------------- | ----------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------ |
| **Internal focus only**                        | Value Chain Analysis looks exclusively inward. It tells you nothing about competitive forces, market attractiveness, or external threats.                                     | Combine with **Porter's Five Forces** (external analysis). Together they form the basis for a comprehensive **SWOT Analysis**. |
| **Finance treated as a "catch-all"**           | Firm Infrastructure groups Finance, Legal, and Planning together — potentially under-emphasizing Finance's strategic role                                                     | Treat Finance as a separate analytical area in your assessment                                                                 |
| **Pre-digital origins**                        | The model was developed before predominantly digital businesses and the internet. While adaptable, the fit for platform businesses, SaaS, or marketplace models is imperfect. | Apply judgment for digital contexts — especially where the "product" is data or software                                       |
| **Static snapshot**                            | Value Chain Analysis reflects a point in time. As technology, markets, and competition shift, the analysis can quickly become outdated                                        | Repeat the analysis annually or after major strategic shifts                                                                   |
| **Activity-level detail can obscure strategy** | Breaking operations into granular sub-activities can cause teams to lose sight of strategic objectives                                                                        | Always tie findings back to the strategic question: cost or differentiation advantage?                                         |

> _"As Value Chain is exclusively an internal framework, it is complemented well by frameworks that are exclusively external such as Five Forces. Once both Five Forces and Value Chain are complete, your SWOT Analysis will likely come out more comprehensive and useful too."_
> _(Source: Lucidity — Porter's Value Chain, May 2025)_

## Framework Overview

> *"The Three Horizons framework provides a structure for companies to assess potential opportunities for growth without neglecting performance in the present."*
> — Steve Coley, McKinsey Director Emeritus
> *(Source: McKinsey.com — Enduring Ideas: The Three Horizons of Growth)*

**Origin:**
The Three Horizons model was popularized by McKinsey & Company consultants Mehrdad Baghai, Stephen Coley, and David White in the late 1990s. It was presented in their book *The Alchemy of Growth* (1999), which codified how companies can systematically manage for both performance today and growth tomorrow. The framework responded to a common problem: successful incumbents tended to optimize the core business (Horizon 1) while underinvesting in emerging businesses (Horizon 2) and future options (Horizon 3).

**The research foundation:**
The book was based on a study of 40 growth companies, where the authors tried to identify how these successful companies approach and implement growth strategies. The key pattern identified is one of a step-by-step approach, a "staircase of initiatives." Companies certainly keep an eye on the longer term strategy, but also simultaneously manage the near term. Each time a short-term target is reached, a new capability is developed, a small acquisition is integrated — successful growers look at this as a platform for the next step.

**The core finding:**
According to McKinsey research published in *The Alchemy of Growth*, companies that managed all three horizons grew revenues 1.6x faster than those that focused solely on their core business.

## The Three Horizons at a Glance

| | **Horizon 1** | **Horizon 2** | **Horizon 3** |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Focus** | Defend & Extend Core | Build Emerging Engines | Create Future Options |
| **Time Frame** | 0–2 years | 2–5 years | 5–10 years |
| **Nature** | Incremental improvement | Adjacent expansion | Disruptive exploration |
| **Profit Profile** | Positive & high margin | Breakeven or early positive | Negative (investment phase) |
| **Risk Level** | Low | Medium | High |
| **Management Style** | Operational discipline | Entrepreneurial scaling | Experimental and exploratory |
| **Resource Allocation** | ~70% | ~20% | ~10% |
| **Metrics** | Revenue, margin, efficiency | Growth rate, market share | Learning, options created |

> *(Source: ThinkInsights — Three Horizons of Growth; Hacking the Case Interview, March 2026; Umbrex, February 2026)*

## Critical Warning — The "Time Collapse" Problem

> In recent years, critics like Steve Blank have argued that the traditional McKinsey model is partially outdated because the "time" element has collapsed. In the digital age, a Horizon 3 disruption (like Generative AI) can move from a "future option" to a "core threat" in months rather than decades. Today, successful firms do not view these horizons as a sequence where one ends and another begins. Instead, they operate them concurrently.

> In the original book, the authors pointed out that every company needs to decide what the right timing for each Horizon should be. For example, some forest product companies were looking at Horizon 3 being 10–20 years away while semiconductor companies looked at Horizon 3 being 18 months away.

**Practical implication:** Do not treat the time frames as fixed. Define them based on your industry's pace of change. The horizons are about **type of initiative**, not just time.

## Horizon 2 — Build Emerging Engines

> Horizon 2 relates to your organization's emerging businesses or products. These are still in their early stages, and require investment or research to thrive.

**The management imperative:**
H2 requires **entrepreneurial energy** combined with **structured oversight**. These businesses are moving from concept to commercial viability — they need enough freedom to experiment but enough accountability to scale.

> The key challenge in H2: these businesses are not yet profitable enough to be self-sustaining, but are too developed to be treated as pure exploration. They require active investment, dedicated leadership, and protection from being cannibalized by H1's operational demands.

## Horizon 3 — Create Future Options

**The management imperative:**
H3 requires **exploration and experimentation** — not operational metrics. The goal is to create *options*, not immediate returns. H3 initiatives should be protected from being measured by H1 metrics.

> Creating a culture that embraces risk and experimentation in H3 can be difficult for organizations with deeply entrenched cultures of stability and predictability.

> **The most dangerous H3 mistake:** applying H1 management discipline to H3 initiatives. Measuring an R&D exploration by quarterly revenue targets kills experimentation before it can generate insight.
> *(Source: TheDigitalBunch — Three Horizons Framework, March 2026)*

## PART 4 — RESOURCE ALLOCATION

---

## Step 4 — The 70-20-10 Allocation Framework

> Allocate resources according to the 70/20/10 rule — 70% to Horizon 1, 20% to Horizon 2, and 10% to Horizon 3.

> **Critical caveat:** The 70-20-10 split provides useful guidance but shouldn't become rigid doctrine. Different organizations face different circumstances requiring adjusted allocations. A startup might invest more heavily in Horizon 2 and 3 while an established industry leader might maintain larger Horizon 1 focus. Market conditions also demand flexibility. Rapid industry change might justify increased Horizon 2 and 3 investment. The framework's value lies in making resource allocation explicit and intentional, not in hitting specific percentage targets.

## PART 5 — THE STAIRCASE OF INITIATIVES

---

## Step 5 — Map the Migration Path

> Successful growers see each milestone — a new capability developed, a small acquisition integrated — as a platform for the next step.

**The Staircase logic:**
H3 initiatives, if successful, migrate to H2. H2 initiatives, if successful, migrate to H1. H1 initiatives that are declining get sunset. This continuous migration ensures the organization always has a pipeline of future growth without abandoning the present.

## PART 6 — MANAGEMENT CHALLENGES BY HORIZON

---

## Different Horizons Require Different Management Approaches

> Each horizon has a different focus, so you will need a different management style for each one.

---

### H1 Management Requirements

| Dimension | What H1 Requires |
|---|---|
| **Leadership style** | Operational discipline, process optimization, accountability for results |
| **Decision speed** | Fast — operational decisions should be delegated; escalate only exceptions |
| **Metrics** | Revenue, margin, market share, customer retention, cost efficiency |
| **Culture** | Execution-focused; zero tolerance for waste; continuous improvement |
| **Talent profile** | Experienced operators; process managers; efficiency experts |
| **Planning horizon** | Quarterly and annual targets; monthly KPI tracking |

---

### H2 Management Requirements

| Dimension | What H2 Requires |
|---|---|
| **Leadership style** | Entrepreneurial energy + structured accountability; stage-gate oversight |
| **Decision speed** | Medium — major pivots need senior sign-off; execution is delegated |
| **Metrics** | Customer acquisition, growth rate, unit economics, path to profitability |
| **Culture** | Startup-like urgency within corporate resources; tolerance for iteration |
| **Talent profile** | Builders and scalers; people who can operate with ambiguity |
| **Planning horizon** | Annual strategy; quarterly milestones; real-time market feedback |
| **Protection needed** | Shield from H1 operational demands; separate P&L if possible |

---

### H3 Management Requirements

| Dimension | What H3 Requires |
|---|---|
| **Leadership style** | Exploratory; hypothesis-driven; comfortable with failure as learning |
| **Decision speed** | Slow for major bets; fast for experiments |
| **Metrics** | Learning validated, assumptions tested, options created — NOT revenue |
| **Culture** | Psychological safety; experimentation; no penalty for honest failure |
| **Talent profile** | Researchers, innovators, entrepreneurs; people who thrive in ambiguity |
| **Planning horizon** | Multi-year vision; quarterly learning reviews; annual portfolio review |
| **Protection needed** | Ring-fenced budget; protected from H1 quarterly pressure at all costs |

> **The most common management failure:** Using H1 metrics (revenue, margin) to evaluate H3 initiatives. This kills exploration before it can yield insight.
> *(Source: TheDigitalBunch — Three Horizons Framework, March 2026; Creately, 2025)*

## PART 7 — COMMON FAILURE MODES

---

## Why Three Horizons Initiatives Fail

> *(Sources: Creately; TheDigitalBunch, March 2026; FourWeekMBA; Umbrex, February 2026)*

| Failure Mode | What Goes Wrong | How to Avoid |
|---|---|---|
| **H1 cannibalism** | Quarterly pressure causes H2 and H3 budgets to be raided for H1 shortfalls. H2 and H3 are treated as discretionary. | Ring-fence H2 and H3 budgets. Make reallocation require CEO/Board approval. |
| **Wrong metrics applied to wrong horizon** | H3 initiatives measured on revenue and ROI — they fail the test every time and get cancelled. | Define horizon-specific success metrics before launching. H3 success = learning, not revenue. |
| **No named owner** | H2 and H3 initiatives are "owned" by committees or added to busy H1 leaders' plates. They never get focused attention. | Every horizon initiative needs a named leader whose primary job it is — not a side project. |
| **Treating time frames as fixed** | Forest product companies applying semiconductor timelines — or vice versa. | Calibrate horizons to your industry's pace of change *(Steve Blank's core criticism)*. |
| **Organizational silos** | Different horizons managed by separate teams or departments, leading to a lack of coordination and potential conflicts in resource allocation. | Create formal cross-horizon governance; senior leadership reviews all three simultaneously. |
| **No H3 at all** | Organization has 100% H1 initiatives. No pipeline. Vulnerable to disruption with no options to respond. | Mandate at least 5–10% exploration — even if returns are uncertain. |
| **H3 theater** | H3 initiatives exist on paper but receive no real resources, executive attention, or permission to fail. | Test: does your H3 leader have a protected budget and direct access to senior leadership? |

## Three Horizons in Practice

| Company | H1 (Core) | H2 (Emerging) | H3 (Future Options) |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Google / Alphabet** | Search advertising (70%+ of revenue) | Google Cloud, YouTube monetization, Android ecosystem | Waymo (self-driving), DeepMind, Loon (now sunset), quantum computing |
| **Amazon** | E-commerce marketplace | AWS (now H1), Prime, Advertising | Amazon Go (cashierless retail), Alexa ecosystem, satellite internet (Kuiper) |
| **Apple** | iPhone (majority of revenue) | Services (App Store, Apple TV+, Apple Music) | Apple Vision Pro (spatial computing), health monitoring, autonomous systems |
| **Tesla** | EV manufacturing and sales | Energy storage (Powerwall, Megapack), Solar | Robotaxi (Full Self-Driving), Optimus humanoid robot, AI chips |
| **Microsoft** | Windows, Office 365 | Azure cloud platform, LinkedIn, Gaming (Xbox) | Generative AI (OpenAI investment), HoloLens (mixed reality), Quantum computing |
| **Nokia (cautionary tale)** | Feature phones (100% focus) | No meaningful H2 | No H3 investment | Lost to iPhone and Android disruption with no pipeline to respond |

> **The Nokia lesson:** 100% H1 focus with no H2 or H3 investment leaves an organization with no options when disruption arrives. Nokia saw the smartphone threat but had no developed H2 or H3 to pivot to.
> *(Source: Strategic Management Insight, May 2025; Hacking the Case Interview, March 2026)*

## Framework Limitations

> *(Sources: Steve Blank — The Fatal Flaw of the Three Horizons Model; BSC Designer; FourWeekMBA; Creately)*

| Limitation | Detail | What to Use Alongside |
|---|---|---|
| **Time frames are industry-relative** | The authors pointed out that every company needs to decide what the right timing for each Horizon should be. Apply your industry's pace of change, not the default numbers. | Combine with **PESTLE** to assess external disruption speed |
| **Digital disruption collapses the timeline** | In the digital age, a Horizon 3 disruption can move from a "future option" to a "core threat" in months rather than decades. | Use **scenario planning** alongside Three Horizons |
| **No guidance on what to put in each horizon** | The framework tells you to invest across horizons — not what specifically to invest in. | Use **Ansoff Matrix** to identify H2 growth directions; **Blue Ocean Strategy** for H3 |
| **Measurement gap in H3** | Measuring success can be challenging, particularly for Horizon 3 initiatives where traditional metrics may not apply. | Define learning milestones and option value, not financial KPIs |
| **Organizational silos** | Managing three different types of business with three different cultures under one roof creates tension and coordination challenges. | Apply **McKinsey 7-S** to ensure organizational alignment for each horizon |
| **Does not address portfolio sequencing** | The framework shows three buckets — not how initiatives flow between them. | Layer the **Staircase of Initiatives** model from *The Alchemy of Growth* |
